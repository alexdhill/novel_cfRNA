# RNA liquid biopsy via nanopore sequencing for novel biomarker discovery and cancer early detection [REVISIONS]

## Abstract

Liquid biopsies detect disease noninvasively by profiling cell-free nucleic acids that are secreted into the circulation. However, existing methods exhibit low sensitivity for detecting early stages of diseases such as cancer. Here we show that long-read nanopore sequencing of long cell-free RNAs in plasma from healthy individuals, precancerous Barrett’s esophagus patients with high-grade dysplasia, or patients with esophageal adenocarcinoma reveals a diverse cell-free RNA transcriptome that can be leveraged for detecting and treating disease. We discovered 286,936 novel, intergenic cell-free RNAs, which we used to build a custom transcriptome reference for quantification, feature selection, and machine learning to accurately classify both precancer and cancer. Moreover, we found potential therapeutic targets, including metabolic, signaling, and immune checkpoint pathways, that are highly upregulated in both precancer and cancer patients. Our findings highlight the utility of our RNA liquid biopsy platform technology for discovering and targeting early stages of disease with molecular precision.

## Background

Esophageal cancer is the sixth leading cause of cancer-related death around the world, with a 5-year survival rate of approximately 20%[cite](shah_improving_2023). An estimated 544,100 people died from esophageal cancer in 2020, and current projections estimate that 880,000 people will die from esophageal cancer in 2040[cite](morgan_global_2022), highlighting the need for better methods to detect and treat esophageal cancer at the earliest signs of disease. Gastroesophageal reflux disease can lead to precancerous Barrett’s esophagus (BE) and dysplasia, which progressively increases the risk of developing esophageal adenocarcinoma (EA)[cite](killcoyne_evolution_2021). However, currently available liquid biopsy methods based on cell-free DNA sequencing detect early-stage cancers with low sensitivity and do not provide comprehensive insights into potential therapeutic targets[cite](liu_sensitive_2020).

Liquid biopsy technologies based on cell-free RNA (cfRNA) sequencing show promise for the detection of certain cancers and precancerous conditions[cite](reggiardo_profiling_2023,roskams-hieter_plasma_2022,anfossi_clinical_2018,lo_biology_2004,hulstaert_charting_2020,larson_comprehensive_2021,loy_liquid_2024,nesselbush_ultrasensitive_2025). cfRNA is actively secreted by cells into the circulation and protected from degradation through encapsulation within extracellular vesicles (EVs): nanometer-sized lipid bilayer particles. Due to their active secretion, EVs reflect the state of their cell of origin in near real-time[cite](welsh_minimal_2024,wang_towards_2021,dixson_context-specific_2023). cfRNAs are comprised of various classes of short and long RNAs, including but not limited to protein-coding RNAs, long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs)[cite](rinn_long_2020,reggiardo_lncrna_2022), and repeat element-derived RNAs[cite](reggiardo_mutant_2022,reggiardo_epigenomic_2020,khojah_extracellular_2022,carrillo_transposable_2023). Although repeat-aware RNA liquid biopsy technologies such as COMPLETE-seq[cite](reggiardo_profiling_2023) enable accurate classification of certain cancer types, including lung, liver, colorectal, and stomach cancers, other cancers such as esophageal cancer exhibit lower sensitivity, highlighting the need for novel approaches that early detection with high sensitivity and specificity.

Current RNA liquid biopsy approaches are constrained by the use of targeted sequencing platforms and/or short-read sequencing technologies, which are unable to RNA sequencing reads longer than a few hundred nucleotides in length. Single-molecule, long-read nanopore sequencing can be leveraged to comprehensively characterize the vast, unannotated cell-free RNA transcriptome across healthy, precancerous, and cancerous disease states. By discovering and incorporating over 280,000 cell-free RNAs unannotated in the GENCODE v39 release into a custom transcriptome reference for quantification and machine learning, we demonstrate that cell-free RNAs enable highly accurate early detection of both precancerous Barrett's esophagus and esophageal adenocarcinoma, while providing systemic molecular insights into the progression from precancer to cancer.

## Results

### LOCATE-Seq Platform

We developed a new RNA liquid biopsy platform technology called LOCATE-seq that harnesses long-read sequencing of cell-free RNA to annotate and quantify both known and novel transcripts to enable disease detection and monitoring (Fig. 1a). We isolated cell-free RNA from the plasma of 16 healthy individuals, 12 patients diagnosed with precancerous Barrett’s esophagus with high-grade dysplasia, and 19 patients diagnosed with early-stage esophageal adenocarcinoma (n=13 stage I, n=5 stage II, n=1 stage III) (Table 1). We then generated cDNA libraries from each sample that were split to make matched sequencing libraries for long-read nanopore sequencing on the PromethION 48 sequencer and short-read sequencing on the Illumina NovaSeq. For novel transcript discovery, we compiled all 47 nanopore sequencing libraries (40.7 million total reads), which revealed 286,936 transcripts not included in the GENCODE v39 annotation of the human transcriptome. Intersection of the novel cfRNAs with GENCODE's annotated poly-A regions show that only 4,188 (1.49%) of the novel cfRNAs overlap poly-A genomic DNA, indicating a low possibility that the identified transcripts are derived from genomic DNA contamination. Short-read libraries for each sample had read support in 283,041 (98.6%) of the long-read derived novel cfRNAs.

We generated a novel-aware transcriptome reference by combining our novel cfRNA transcripts with all of the known transcripts in GENCODE v39, substantially expanding the number of cfRNAs we are able to quantify. To  deeply profile our healthy and patient plasma samples we performed high-depth, short-read sequencing of our cfRNA libraries on the NovaSeq X sequencer (1.26 billion total reads) and mapped them to the GENCODE transcriptome reference and our custom cfRNA-aware transcriptome reference (GENCODE + Novel cfRNA). The median numbers of detected protein-coding genes were comparable whether we used the GENCODE transcriptome reference (healthy: 13,772, dysplasia: 12,342, cancer: 13,388) or our custom GENCODE + novel cfRNA transcriptome reference (healthy: 13,779, dysplasia: 11,892, cancer: 13,046) for cfRNA quantification (Fig. 1b). Median lncRNA genes detected were also comparable between GENCODE (healthy: 6,964, dysplasia: 5,540, cancer: 6,256) and GENCODE + novel cfRNA (healthy: 6,673, dysplasia: 5,074, cancer: 5,797) quantification (Fig. 1c). Though there was a small decrease in the number of detected genes when using the GENCODE + novel cfRNA transcriptome, the quantifications remained highly similar (R2 = 1) indicating that the quantifications were not significantly affected by the addition of the novel cfRNAs to the transcriptome (Fig. S1). For novel cfRNAs, the number of detected genes was highest in healthy individuals (median: 157,821) when compared to dysplasia (median: 115,184) and cancer (median: 143,431) patients (Fig. 1d). The changes in gene detecetion observed between conditions roughly followed the sizes of the libraries generated. Novel cfRNAs were distributed relatively evenly throughout the genome (Fig. 1e), with the majority of novel cfRNAs being less than 1,000 base pairs (bp) in length (Fig. 1f).

### Differential expression in dysplasia and cancer

Quantification and differential expression (DE) analysis of the short-read libraries revealed significant enrichment of mitochondrial and protein-coding RNA in precancerous high-grade dysplasia (Fig. 2a), and esophageal adenocarcinoma (Fig. 2b) when compared to healthy individuals. Mitochondrial RNAs were among the most significantly enriched molecular signature across both DE analyses, indicating that mitochondrial cfRNAs that are strongly secreted at the precancerous state and continue to persist in esophageal cancer. many protein-coding RNAs were also commonly enriched across each of the DE analyses (Fig. 2c), while lncRNAs were the most commonly depleted across each of the comparisons (Fig. 2d). Significantly more mitochondrial RNAs were represented in the cfRNA of patients with precancerous dysplasia or cancer (Fig. 2e), suggesting that mitochondrial RNAs may serve as highly abundant biomarkers for both precancer and cancer early detection. Using the expression of the mitochondiral genes, the healthy patients were almost perfectly segregated from the high-grade dysplasia and esophageal adenocarcinoma through hierarchical clustering of the cfRNA counts (Fig. 2f).

### Novel cfRNA features enable accurate disease classification

To test the diagnostic potential of the cfRNAs in premalignant and early-stage disease, we trained machine learning models using the significantly differentially expressed cfRNAs (or sample metadata) as input features (p-adjusted <= 0.05, |log2FoldChange| >= 1). We trained 4 models on different feature subspaces to highlight the predictive power of the novel cfRNAs in addition to the GENCODE cfRNA counts. First, we showed that the cohort composition did not strongly impact the predictions by training logistic regression models on the age and sex of each patient (Fig. 4aei). Despite the fact that the healthy donors were slightly older on average (Fig. S1b), these models did not show predictive power much greater than random classification, implying that the patient metadata does not contribute strongly to the model performance.

We then trained logistic regression models using either GENCODE (Fig. 4bfj) or novel cfRNAs (Fig. 4cgk), which were able to perfectly classify high-grade dysplasia and esophageal adenocarcinoma patients from healthy samples with perfect sensitivity (100%) and specificity (100%), using only a limited number of genes or novel cfRNAs as features. When GENCODE and novel cfRNAs were combined into one feature space, LASSO regularization kept 11 novel cfRNAs in the 15 features for predicting high-grade dysplasia, and 5 novel cfRNAs in the 13 features for predicting esophageal adenocarcinoma. DE analysis only revealed 29 genes with differential expression between high-grade dysplasia and esophageal adenocarcinoma plasma samples, 25 of which were novel cfRNAs. Using the same approaches as previously described, we trained logistic regression models to segregate esophageal adenocarcinoma and high-grade dysplasia using cell-free RNA counts. Since only 4 annotated genes appeared differentially expressed, the model trained on the GENCODE features did not perform well. In contrast, the model trained on novel cfRNAs again perfect classification between the premalignant and early-stage disease conditions.

## Discussion

Here we show that the comprehensive profiling of cfRNA using nanopore sequencing enables the discovery of unannotated, novel cfRNA transcripts, many of which are differentially expressed in disease and can be leveraged as biomarkers for pre-cancer and cancer early detection with high sensitivity and specificity using machine learning. A multitude of these novel cfRNAs do not appear patient specific, indicating the potential for these RNAs to be to be applicable in a broader clinical context. Our findings reveal that our understanding of the true complexity of cfRNA transcriptomes in the context of health and disease is still nascent, and by using single-molecule, long-read nanopore sequencing of cfRNA, we reveal the dynamic diversity of cfRNA transcriptomes in patients with precancerous high-grade dysplasia or esophageal adenocarcinoma. Our LOCATE-seq RNA liquid biopsy platform technology allows us to profile and harness this rich diversity of cfRNA for novel biomarker discovery and highly accurate disease classification.

Our study reveals that mitochondrial RNAs are highly enriched in the plasma of both precancerous dysplasia and early-stage cancer patients, highlighting their utility as biomarkers of disease and underscoring their role in providing potential insights into metabolic changes and disease biology. A recent study using the PC3 prostate cancer cell line also found that mitochondrial RNAs are enriched in large oncosomes: vesicles greater than 1nm in size that are actively secreted by cancer cells. This data suggests that mitochondrial RNA may serve as circulating biomarkers for various types of cancers[cite](silva_extracellular_2025), and deeper exploration into pan-cancer cohorts are needed to determine whether this may act as a general biomarker of genomic disease. It is possible that this is a reflection of the increased metabolic activity of diseased tissue, or the secretion of whole mitochondria which has been observed in other studies, but further research is needed to truly explore the origins of the extracellular signatures identified. Increased levels of mitochondria-derived cfRNA and enrichment of gene sets such as oxidative phosphorylation in dysplasia and cancer also suggest that potential therapeutic strategies targeting this metabolic pathway may be used for disease treatment.

Our findings also uncover additional pathways which could suggest therapeutic interventions, including enrichment of MYC and PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling pathways in both dysplasia and cancer, suggesting the potential utility of immunotherapy for esophageal cancer prevention. Previous studies have shown that the pH  changes induced by bile acid expsoure leading to the formation of Barrett's esophagus activates both Myc, and the mTOR pathway via IKK$beta$ resulting in cell proliferation and anchorage-independent growth[cite](tselepis_upregulation_2003,yen_bile_2008). Given the advanced age of this cohort it is worth acknowledging that these pathways, along with the enriched interferon response pathways, are potential mutations found in clonal hematopoesis of indeterminant origin (CHIP), however mutations found in these pathways are rare in CHIP and would likely have not appeared as upregulated between conditions, and instead would have been elevated in few individual patients[cite](gaulin_molecular_2022).

The GENCODE annotated and novel cfRNAs that we discovered and incorporated into our custom transcriptome reference were highly predictive of both precancerous high-grade dysplasia and early-stage esophageal adenocarcinoma. Logistic regression models trained on the cfRNA counts were able to perfectly segregate these two disease conditions from healthy donors. Moreover, the novel cfRNAs alone were able to perfectly segregate esophageal adenocarcinoma from high-grade dysplasia, while the GENCODE genes were not able to segregate these two conditions. This highlights the utility of comprehensively profiling the cfRNA transcriptome for biomarker discovery and disease classification, as well as the potential for novel cfRNAs to serve as biomarkers for early detection of disease. The models generated for this study certainly would not perform in a clinical setting as the initial cohort was too small for larger holdouts and cross-validation to take place. This limits the number of holdout samples and can lead to overfitting, however, the perfect performance of the cfRNA models in segregating both high-grade dysplasia and esophageal adenocarcinoma from healthy donors, as well as segregating esophageal adenocarcinoma from high-grade dysplasia, is promising. Once concern with identifying cancer-specific RNA biomarkers is the potential for these biomarkers to be patient-specific, making them clincally useless, however we found that many of the novel cfRNAs were expressed in many of the patients, suggesting that they may be more broadly applicable as biomarkers for early detection of disease. Future studies with larger cohorts and independent validation will be important for validating the expression of our novel cfRNAs, as well as the generalizability and performance of the models.

Taken together, our study provides proof-of-concept that long-read nanopore sequencing of cfRNA reveals a rich diversity of previously unknown cfRNA transcripts that can serve as new biomarkers and novel features for early cancer or potentially pre-cancer detection. Further validation using plasma samples from independent patient cohorts will be important for validating and testing the performance of our models. Our study also demonstrates the advantages of comprehensively profiling the cfRNA transcriptome for drug target discovery and for informing potential therapeutic strategies for cancer treatment and prevention.

## Methods

### Cell-free RNA isolation

Blood plasma from patients with Barrett's esophagus with high-grade dysplasia, or esophageal adenocarcinoma were collected from Cambridge University Hospital. Healthy donors were identified as those without disease by BioIVT sample metadata. Plasma samples were passed through a 0.8µm filter to remove the buffy coat and cellular debris. Cell-free RNA was isolated from the filtered plasma using the Norgen Biotek Plasma/Serum Circulating and Exosomal RNA Purification Kit (slurry format), yielding approximately 2-3 ng of RNA. Isolated RNA was eluted into 25 µl and stored at –80C prior to cDNA synthesis.

### cDNA synthesis

10 uL of RNA was input to the Takara SMART-Seq HT plus kit to generate cDNA libraries. cDNA was prepared as specified by the manufacturer’s protocol and amplified for 24 cycles to ensure enough cDNA was present for downstream sequencing. Quantification of the resultant cDNA was done using both a Nanodrop spectrophotometer, as well as a Qubit Fluorometer. The size distribution of the cDNA was verified using an Agilent Bioanalyzer prior to sequencing library preparation. cDNA libraries were subsequently split and prepared for sequencing on both long- and short-read platforms.

### Long-read sequencing

Barcoded nanopore sequencing libraries were prepared using modified Oxford Nanopore Technologies kits SQK-NBD114.24 and LSK-114. The SQK-NBD114.24 kit was used to barcode cDNA prior to library construction. cDNA underwent end repair and A-tailing according to the manufacturer’s protocol, with modifications that included a 30-minute incubation followed by a 30-minute deactivation step to enhance end-repair and A-tailing efficiency. Barcode ligation was performed using barcodes from the SQK-NBD114.24 kit and incubated at 20 °C for 4.5 hours to ensure efficient ligation. Reactions were terminated by the addition of 2 µL EDTA per sample. Barcoded samples were then pooled and subjected to a 1.8× AMPure bead cleanup to ensure efficient recovery of cDNA, after which the pooled material was used as input for the LSK-114 ligation sequencing protocol. Additional modifications were applied during LSK-114 library preparation, including the use of a 1.8× bead to sample ratio for all cleanup steps and performing all elutions at 37 °C to improve cDNA recovery. Adapter ligation was extended to 20 minutes to increase ligation yield, and Short Fragment Buffer was used to promote retention of full-length cDNA molecules. The final library was eluted in 25 µL of elution buffer. 12 libraries were barcoded and loaded onto a single R10.4 PromethION flow cell, using 4 flowcells for the total experiment. Healthy and disease conditions were distributed between sequencing runs to mitigate batch effects in downstream analyses. Raw POD5 files were basecalled and demultiplexed using Dorado v0.5.3 with the SUPv4.3.0 basecalling model. Reads that failed initial demultiplexing were subsequently reprocessed using a custom Python script that identified exact barcode matches within unclassified reads, which were then reassigned and merged with their corresponding FASTQ files.

### Short-read sequencing

Illumina libraries were prepared using identical cDNA as the long-read sequencing libraries generated from the isolated plasma RNA of healthy, Barrett’s esophagus, and esophageal cancer samples. Libraries were prepared via the Takara SMART-Seq HT plus low-input kit following the manufacturer's protocol and sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq (2×150 bp reads) at UC Santa Cruz.

### Novel transcript discovery

Novel transcript annotations were generated using IsoQuant version 3.3.1 [cite](prjibelski_accurate_2023) with the Human reference genome build HG38 and the GENCODE version 39 comprehensive transcript annotation[cite](frankish_gencode_2021). IsoQuant was run on the concatonation of all 47 nanopore sequencing libraries with the following parameters:

`-d nanopore, –stranded none, --report_novel_unspliced true, –matching_strategy loose, --gene_quantification all, --transcript_quantification all`

Annotated poly-adenosine regions of HG38 were obtained from the GENCODE comprehensive annotation and used to identify novel cfRNAs that could be derived from genomic DNA contamination.

### RNA-seq quality and quantification

RNA-seq long reads were trimmed using Porechop (v0.2.4) and aligned to to the GENCODE v39 and novel cfRNA transcriptome using minimap2 (v2.30) [cite](li_minimap2_2018) with the 'sr' preset which increases the number of alignments when dealing with cfRNA. Transcriptome alignments were quantified by Oarfish (v0.9.0) [cite](zare_jousheghani_oarfish_2025) using the coverage model. Due to the low read count of the long-read data the quantifications were used only to correlate with the more accurate, higher depth short-read quantifications.

RNA-seq short reads (FASTQ) were trimmed using FastP (v0.23.4)[cite](chen_fastp_2018) to remove adapter sequences and low quality bases. Both raw and trimmed reads were assessed using FastQC (v0.12.1), and quantified using Salmon (v1.10.1)[cite](patro_salmon_2017) in mapping mode. To reduce sequence biases, enable selective alignments, rescue reads with an unmapped pair, and improve quantification accuracy, quantification was run with the following parameters:

`--gcBias, --seqBias, --validateMappings, --recoverOrphans, --rangeFactorizationBins 4`

The salmon mappings were performed against two transcriptome indexes:

  1. the HG38 human genome reference annotation (v39) from the GENCODE consortium, and the RepeatMasker track from the University of California Santa Cruz genome browser (refered to as GENCODE)

  2. The above, and an annotation of novel genes identified via IsoQuant (3.3.1) (refered to as GENCODE + novel cfRNA)

Transcriptomes were generated using gffread (v0.12.7)[cite](pertea_gff_2020) to extract the spliced transcript sequences from the HG38 genome reference. The transcriptomes were then indexed using salmon and oarfish for quantification of the short- and long-reads respectively. Transcript counts were aggregated to the gene level for GENCODE genes.

### Short-read quantification analysis

Short-read transcripts were imported into R (v4.3.3)[cite](R) using tximport (v1.22.0)[cite](soneson_differential_2016). Biotypes were assigned to genes using their annotated GENCODE biotypes. IsoQuant annotated novel genes were assigned the ‘Novel gene’ biotype. Count normalization and differential expression (DE) analyses were performed using DESeq2 (v1.42.0)[cite](love_moderated_2014). DE analysis was performed on genes that were expressed with a raw count >=3 in at least 25% of samples to remove low abundance and rarely expressed genes. Significant differential expression was limited to genes with a Benjamini-Hochberg adjusted p-value < 0.05, and an absolute value log2 fold-change >= 1. Gene set enrichment analysis was performed using the fgsea package (v1.28.0)[cite](korotkevich_fast_2019) against the Hallmark genesets from the Molecular Signatures Database (MSigDB)[cite](subramanian_gene_2005,liberzon_molecular_2015), using the Wald statistic from DESeq2. Gene sets with an adjusted p-value < 0.01 were considered significant. All figures were generated using ggplot2[cite](ggplot2) and patchwork[cite](patchwork).

### Modeling

Logistic regression models were trained in R using the glmnet package (v4.1)[cite](glmnet). Binary models were trained to discriminate either high-grade dysplasia from healthy, esophageal adenocarcinoma from healthy, or esophageal adenocarcinoma from high-grade dysplasia. For each binary classifier a model was trained on one of four different feature spaces: metadata only (sex, age), GENCODE genes counts only, novel transcript count only, and a combination of GENCODE and novel transcript counts. This resulted in 12 distinct models (3 classifiers × 4 feature spaces). GENCODE, novel cfRNAs, and the combined feature spaces were only provided differentially expressed genes to reduce noise and speed up model training.

Each model was trained in three passes: the first pass used 4-fold cross validation across a range of training parameters (lambda) and LASSO regularization to identify uninformative features. The lambda value with the lowest average mean squared error was selected, and features with a non-zero coefficient were retained for subsequent passes. The second pass used 4-fold cross validation to train over a grid of alpha and lambda values to tune the elastic-net mixing and learning rates. The combination of alpha and lambda values with the lowest mean squared error was used to train the final model. The final model for each classifier were trained on the same 66% of the original dataset. Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves and area under the curve (AUC) values were calculated using the pROC package (v1.18.0)[cite](pROC) against the 34% of the samples held-out from training.
